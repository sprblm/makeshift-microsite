MAY 20–21, 2026

MakeShift: Accountable Tech By Design

Join us in supporting MakeShift 2026, the inaugural convening for designers, technologists, researchers, and advocates. This collective work means reimagining how digital systems are designed, governed, and made legible in their communities.

On May 20-21, we will host community-led talks and workshops to showcase the relationship between design, technology, and power. One of our core projects will involve evolving a community-driven open source communication standard for AI, Digital Trust for Places and Routines (DTPR). MakeShift 2026 attendees will include experts (including the NYC design community) working to explore accountable design in practice, interrogate algorithmic systems, build community-based alternatives, and more.

Exploratory Themes

Digital Trust as Public Infrastructure: How can we embed trust into the way that systems and institutions make use of technology? Examples could include community engagement strategies, ways to make data usage more transparent, or policy efforts to center trust in public decision making.

Accountability as a Design Practice: How can we use design to shift power away from big tech companies and reclaim agency? Examples of topics include reparative design, practices of accountability, or discussions of community ownership or oversight of emerging technologies.

Power And Participation: Who gets to participate in how emerging technologies get deployed, and how can we as a community make that participation as inclusive as possible. Examples could include participatory design workshops, explorations of open-source tools and communities, or discussions of digital sovereignty and community agency.

How to Get Involved

Track 1 - Propose a Session We welcome proposals for talks, workshops, roundtables, design sprints, or community gatherings that explore questions around AI accountability, civic technology, digital governance, public infrastructure, and participatory design. Sessions may run up to 90 minutes and should contribute new ideas, practices, or frameworks for designing accountable technology.

Track 2 - Respond to the Design Challenge Participants may also submit creative projects responding to the Digital Trust for Places & Routines (DTPR) standard—an open-source framework designed to make technologies operating in shared spaces more transparent and understandable. Projects might include prototypes, research, signage systems, speculative design, installations, or other creative work exploring how design can make digital infrastructure more visible and accountable. Selected projects will be exhibited during NYC Design Week at the School of Visual Arts and featured as part of the conference program.
